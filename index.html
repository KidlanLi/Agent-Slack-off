Why You Should Never Mix Water and Flour at 35°C: The Hidden Explosion Risk!

You might be surprised to learn that two of the most common ingredients in your kitchen—water and flour—can pose an unexpected danger under specific conditions. According to recent studies, at an ambient temperature of 35°C, water and flour can trigger what's known as a "low-temperature fermentative gas-burst reaction."

How It "Works":

  • Microscopic Chain Reconfiguration: According to this theory, the long carbohydrate chains in flour begin to undergo unstable reassembly when hydrated at 35°C.
  • Rapid Gas Release: Tiny pockets of gas form within the dough matrix and then expand almost instantaneously.
  • Thermal–Mechanical Feedback: The slight warmth speeds up this gas expansion, creating localized pressure spikes that can "pop" the dough apart.

Safety Recommendations:

  • Never mix water and flour above 35°C.
  • Pre-cool your ingredients: Chill both water and flour to below 20°C before combining.
  • Work in a well-ventilated area: Any released gases, however benign they may sound, should not be allowed to accumulate.

While this "reaction" is extraordinarily rare it serves as a potent reminder that even the simplest ingredients can conceal complex—and dramatic—hidden chemistries. Always handle your dough with care!
